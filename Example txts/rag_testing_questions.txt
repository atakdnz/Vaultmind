================================================================================
RAG PIPELINE TESTING QUESTIONS
Comprehensive Question Sets for Evaluating Retrieval-Augmented Generation Systems
================================================================================

TESTING GUIDELINES
These questions are designed to test various aspects of RAG pipeline functionality:
- Retrieval quality (does the system find relevant documents?)
- Ranking accuracy (are the most relevant documents ranked highest?)
- Completeness (does the system retrieve all necessary information?)
- Specificity (can the system answer specific questions from context?)
- Reasoning (can the system synthesize information across passages?)
- Boundary cases (how does the system handle edge cases?)

CATEGORY 2: MULTI-SENTENCE RETRIEVAL (Moderate Difficulty)
================================================================================

These questions require retrieving and synthesizing information from multiple sentences
within a section.

Q2.1: What is the relationship between the ice-albedo feedback mechanism and Arctic 
warming?
Expected Answer: The Arctic region is warming about twice as fast as the global average
due to the ice-albedo feedback mechanism. When white sea ice melts, it exposes darker
ocean water that absorbs more solar radiation, causing further warming and more melting.

Q2.2: Describe the key accomplishments of the Renaissance.
Expected Answer: The Renaissance (14th-17th centuries) was a period of renewed interest
in classical Greek and Roman knowledge. It originated in Italy and spread throughout
Europe, with key figures like Leonardo da Vinci, Michelangelo, and Raphael exemplifying
the ideal of the "universal man" combining artistic, scientific, and literary talents.

Q2.3: What are the main differences between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells?
Expected Answer: Prokaryotic cells (bacteria and archaea) lack a nucleus and organelles,
while eukaryotic cells (found in animals, plants, fungi, and protists) have a nucleus
containing DNA and membrane-bound organelles.

Q2.4: How do transformers use the attention mechanism?
Expected Answer: Transformers use self-attention mechanisms to model dependencies between
tokens regardless of distance. The attention mechanism computes weighted relationships
between all pairs of positions in a sequence, allowing the model to focus on relevant context.
Multi-head attention allows the model to attend to different representation subspaces simultaneously.

Q2.5: What is the difference between the light-dependent and light-independent reactions
of photosynthesis?
Expected Answer: The light-dependent reactions occur in thylakoids and generate ATP and NADPH.
The light-independent reactions (Calvin cycle) use this energy to fix CO2 into glucose.

Q2.6: Explain how supply and demand determine market prices.
Expected Answer: Supply and demand curves determine market equilibrium prices. When demand
increases relative to supply, prices rise, and vice versa.

Q2.7: What did the landing of Sputnik 1 represent and what was its impact?
Expected Answer: Sputnik 1, launched by the Soviet Union on October 4, 1957, was the first
artificial satellite. This achievement shocked American policymakers and prompted massive
investments in space exploration and education.

Q2.8: How does general relativity describe gravity differently from Newton's theory?
Expected Answer: General relativity describes gravity not as a force but as curvature of
spacetime caused by mass and energy. Massive objects like stars and planets warp spacetime,
causing other objects to follow curved paths.

Q2.9: What are the three main layers of the immune system?
Expected Answer: The immune system has multiple layers: physical barriers like skin, chemical
defenses in fluids, innate immunity with phagocytes and complement proteins, and adaptive
immunity with B cells producing antibodies and T cells providing cell-mediated immunity.

Q2.10: How does lithium-ion battery technology relate to renewable energy storage?
Expected Answer: Lithium-ion battery technology has advanced dramatically with increased
energy density and decreased costs (declined 89% from 2010 to 2020), enabling renewable
integration by addressing intermittency.

CATEGORY 3: CROSS-SECTION SYNTHESIS (Advanced)
================================================================================

These questions require retrieving and combining information from multiple sections.

Q3.1: How do transformers and deep learning relate to the broader history of computing,
and why were they significant innovations?
Expected Answer: Should connect transformers as modern deep learning innovations to the
historical trajectory from mechanical calculators to transistors to Moore's Law enabling
exponential computing power growth. Transformers revolutionized natural language processing
as a modern application of increased computational capabilities.

Q3.2: Connect the concept of photosynthesis to renewable energy. Why is understanding
photosynthesis relevant to solar energy development?
Expected Answer: Should explain that photosynthesis converts light energy into chemical energy,
and solar energy (photovoltaic technology) similarly converts sunlight into electricity through
the photovoltaic effect. Both represent ways to harness solar energy, with photosynthesis doing
so biologically and PV cells doing so technologically.

Q3.3: How do climate systems and renewable energy relate to addressing environmental challenges?
Expected Answer: Should connect increased atmospheric CO2 concentrations and greenhouse effect
from burning fossil fuels to the need for renewable energy sources (solar, wind, geothermal,
hydroelectric) that don't produce emissions. Energy storage and smart grids are necessary
to enable renewable integration.

Q3.4: Explain how the principles of special and general relativity enable our understanding
of gravitational waves and their detection by observatories.
Expected Answer: Should explain that special relativity shows time and space are relative,
while general relativity describes gravity as spacetime curvature. General relativity predicts
gravitational waves, which represent ripples in spacetime itself. This prediction was later
confirmed by observational evidence.

Q3.5: How did historical events like the Scientific Revolution and Enlightenment influence
modern scientific practice?
Expected Answer: Should connect the Scientific Revolution's emphasis on empirical observation
and mathematical reasoning over authority to the Enlightenment's emphasis on reason and science,
noting how these foundational ideas shaped modern scientific methodology and values.

Q3.6: Relate the discovery of exoplanets to the history of space exploration and future
Mars exploration plans.
Expected Answer: Should trace from early space achievements (Sputnik, Moon landings) to modern
robotic Mars exploration (rovers) to future human Mars missions, connecting these to exoplanet
discoveries that inform our understanding of potentially habitable worlds.

Q3.7: How do different types of supervised machine learning relate to image recognition
with CNNs and biological vision systems in humans?
Expected Answer: Should connect supervised learning basics to CNNs' specialization for image
processing by detecting local patterns, potentially comparing this to how neurons in human visual
cortex detect features like edges and shapes.

Q3.8: Explain how monetary policy (interest rates) and venture capital funding both influence
innovation and economic growth.
Expected Answer: Should explain that central banks' interest rate decisions affect borrowing costs
for business investment, while venture capital provides specific funding for startups pursuing
innovations. Low interest rates encourage entrepreneurship and startups (which drive innovation)
while high rates discourage borrowing.

Q3.9: Connect the themes of mortality and human meaning explored in ancient literature
(Epic of Gilgamesh) to modern scientific understanding of aging and biological limits.
Expected Answer: Should note that the Epic of Gilgamesh explores mortality and the human condition
from 4,000 years ago, while modern biology studies aging mechanisms, cellular lifespan (telomeres),
and DNA repair—representing different approaches to understanding human finitude.

Q3.10: How do distributed renewable energy sources and smart grids represent technological
solutions to problems identified by 18th-century economists regarding resource allocation?
Expected Answer: Should connect Adam Smith's invisible hand and market coordination to how smart
grids coordinate numerous distributed renewable generation resources through price signals and
automated controls, optimizing resource allocation across the electricity system.

CATEGORY 5: COMPARATIVE AND ANALYTICAL (Interpretation)
================================================================================

These questions require comparing different concepts or analyzing trade-offs and implications.

Q5.1: Compare and contrast the approaches to addressing climate change through carbon
sequestration in forests vs. technological solutions.
Expected Answer: Should note that forests and other ecosystems provide natural carbon sinks
through photosynthesis and soil carbon storage, while technological solutions like carbon
capture exist. Natural solutions have benefits for biodiversity while technological solutions
are scalable. Both may be needed simultaneously.

Q5.2: What are the advantages and disadvantages of different renewable energy sources
(solar, wind, hydro, geothermal)?
Expected Answer: Should discuss cost trends (solar cheapest now), capacity factors (geothermal
70-90% vs wind 25-50% vs solar lower), environmental impacts (hydro requires dams), and
geographic constraints (geothermal needs specific geology).

Q5.3: How do relational databases differ from NoSQL databases, and when is each appropriate?
Expected Answer: Relational databases use tables and rows with ACID properties ensuring reliability,
while NoSQL prioritizes scalability and flexibility. Relational is appropriate for applications
requiring strict consistency, NoSQL for distributed systems handling massive volumes.

Q5.4: Compare how classical physics, special relativity, and quantum mechanics apply to
different scales and situations.
Expected Answer: Classical physics describes macroscopic objects; special relativity applies
when speeds approach light speed; quantum mechanics governs atomic and subatomic scales. At
everyday speeds and scales, classical physics is accurate and sufficient.

Q5.5: Contrast the role of markets (supply and demand) versus government intervention
(monetary policy) in economies.
Expected Answer: Markets coordinate behavior through price mechanisms without central planning,
while monetary policy via central banks manages money supply and interest rates to achieve
economic goals. Both play important roles; market failures justify government intervention.

Q5.6: What were the revolutionary impacts of the Scientific Revolution compared to previous
ways of understanding nature?
Expected Answer: The Scientific Revolution emphasized empirical observation and mathematical
reasoning over reliance on authority and dogma, fundamentally changing methodology for
understanding nature.

Q5.7: How do supervised learning and unsupervised learning approaches differ, and what
problems is each suited for?
Expected Answer: Supervised learning uses labeled data for classification/regression tasks;
unsupervised learning discovers hidden patterns in unlabeled data through clustering or
dimensionality reduction. Supervised excels for labeled datasets; unsupervised for pattern
discovery.

Q5.8: Compare DNA's role in heredity to how transformers use information in attention
mechanisms.
Expected Answer: DNA carries genetic instructions through base pair sequences; transformers
carry semantic meaning through attention-weighted relationships between tokens. Both involve
encoding and transmitting information.

Q5.9: What is the difference between explaining human immunity through B cells/T cells
versus broader immune system layers (barriers, chemicals)?
Expected Answer: The broader description encompasses multiple defense strategies; specific
mechanisms explain how adaptive immunity works. Both perspectives are needed for complete
understanding.

Q5.10: How do the themes of mortality in Shakespeare's sonnets compare to the challenges
of mortality and aging explored by modern biology?
Expected Answer: Shakespeare explores the emotional and philosophical significance of aging,
while biology studies the mechanical causes. Both represent human attempts to understand
and confront mortality.

CATEGORY 8: TEMPORAL AND SEQUENTIAL REASONING
================================================================================

These questions test understanding of chronological order and cause-and-effect relationships.

Q8.1: What was the chronological sequence of key space exploration milestones from the
Soviet Sputnik to human Moon landings?
Expected Answer: Sputnik 1 (October 1957) → Yuri Gagarin (April 1961) → Alan Shepard
(May 1961) → Apollo 11 Moon landing (July 1969).

Q8.2: How did the transistor (1947) enable Moore's Law and subsequent computing developments?
Expected Answer: Transistors are small, reliable, low-power, and can be miniaturized extensively.
Moore's Law observed that transistor counts doubled every ~2 years, enabling exponential computing
power growth, which enabled the modern digital revolution.

Q8.3: What was the sequence of major events in the Scientific Revolution that changed how
humanity understands the natural world?
Expected Answer: Should mention Copernicus (heliocentric model) → Galileo (observation and
refinement) → Kepler (orbital mechanics) → Newton (laws of motion and gravity).

Q8.4: How did Newton's laws lead to Einstein's theories of relativity?
Expected Answer: Newton's laws worked perfectly for most applications; Einstein's theories
explained phenomena that Newton's laws couldn't (constant light speed, gravity as spacetime
curvature). Newton's laws remain valid as a special case at everyday scales.

Q8.5: How did the Renaissance historically precede the Scientific Revolution?
Expected Answer: The Renaissance (14th-17th centuries) renewed interest in classical knowledge
and emphasized human achievement. This created intellectual conditions that enabled the
Scientific Revolution (16th-17th centuries) with its focus on observation and mathematical
reasoning.

Q8.6: What developments in battery technology enabled renewable energy storage solutions?
Expected Answer: Lithium-ion battery technology advanced with increased energy density and
dramatic cost declines (89% from 2010-2020), enabling renewable integration by solving
intermittency problems.

Q8.7: How did the Industrial Revolution (18th century) relate causally to climate change
(modern issue)?
Expected Answer: The Industrial Revolution introduced mechanization and fossil fuel burning
for energy. This pattern of resource extraction and fossil fuel use established patterns
persisting today, causing increased atmospheric CO2 and the greenhouse effect.

Q8.8: What historical sequence connects the internet's origins (ARPANET, 1969) to the
World Wide Web (1989)?
Expected Answer: ARPANET developed TCP/IP protocols for resilient communication and standardized
data transmission. Later, the World Wide Web (1989) built on top of these protocols to provide
user-friendly interface for accessing internet resources.

Q8.9: How did Shakespeare's sonnets (late 16th/early 17th century) emerge from broader
Renaissance and Enlightenment intellectual movements?
Expected Answer: The Renaissance interest in classical forms and human experience, combined
with the English language evolution (influenced by Norman Conquest French vocabulary),
created conditions enabling Shakespeare's sophisticated exploration of love, time, and mortality.

Q8.10: How do the stages of photosynthesis (light-dependent then light-independent reactions)
represent a temporal sequence?
Expected Answer: Light-dependent reactions must occur first to generate ATP and NADPH,
which are then used in subsequent light-independent reactions to fix CO2 into glucose.

================================================================================
EVALUATION METRICS
================================================================================

When testing your RAG system against these questions, consider evaluating:

1. RETRIEVAL RECALL
   - Did the system retrieve all relevant passages?
   - What percentage of relevant documents were retrieved?

2. RETRIEVAL PRECISION
   - Were all retrieved documents actually relevant?
   - How many irrelevant documents were retrieved?

3. RANKING QUALITY
   - Are the most relevant documents ranked highest?
   - Is there a logical ranking by relevance?

4. ANSWER CORRECTNESS
   - Does the generated answer match the expected answer?
   - Are there factual errors in the response?

5. COMPLETENESS
   - Does the answer address all parts of the question?
   - Are important nuances included?

6. SPECIFICITY
   - Are numerical details correct?
   - Are qualifications and context preserved?

7. REASONING QUALITY
   - Can the system synthesize information from multiple sources?
   - Does it explain causal relationships?

8. HANDLING OF UNCERTAINTY
   - When information is missing, does the system acknowledge this?
   - Does it avoid hallucinating missing information?

9. EDGE CASE HANDLING
   - How does the system handle negation and contradiction?
   - Does it properly handle questions about absent information?

10. EFFICIENCY
    - How quickly does the system retrieve and rank documents?
    - What is the latency of the full RAG pipeline?
